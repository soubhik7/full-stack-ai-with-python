# Chapter 7 — Planning the Transition

> Source: *The Agentic SDLC Handbook* (Daniel Meppiel, v0.9.2), pp. 82–93.
> A successful small pilot does not generalize into a successful organization-wide rollout by itself — this chapter is the operational bridge that turns the strategic case for agentic development into a phased, measurable, reversible rollout plan.

---

## Very Important

### The Productivity Paradox and Why Readiness, Not Output, Is the Planning Unit
The Productivity Paradox (introduced in Chapter 3) means traditional velocity and lines-of-code metrics become misleading during AI-assisted transition. Teams that measure success by code output will misdiagnose the adoption valley as failure. The chapter's metrics are deliberately designed around outcomes (quality, review throughput, escalation patterns) rather than output volume: **do not plan your transition around a productivity number — plan it around readiness level, your organization's ability to use these tools reliably and at scale.**

The chapter opens with a representative failure pattern: a successful two-week pilot with three enthusiastic developers on a greenfield microservice leads to a wider rollout; six months later, adoption is uneven — two teams swear by it, three reverted to their previous workflow, one team produces code faster but spends more time in review because no one trusts the output, costs are climbing, and the metrics are ambiguous. This is not failure — it is something worse: **inconclusive results that neither justify expanding the investment nor provide grounds for canceling it.** The root cause: organizations skip from "successful pilot" to "organization-wide rollout" without building the infrastructure that makes agentic development reliable at scale — context engineering, governance, skill development, measurement systems. The pilot succeeds *because* it's small (limited codebase, enthusiastic participants, greenfield scope); the rollout fails because none of those conditions hold for the rest of the organization.

### Team Readiness Assessment
Not every team is equally ready for agentic development, and not every team should start at the same time. The assessment prevents the common mistake of rolling out uniformly where conditions vary dramatically. Four dimensions, each scored **Not Ready / Partially Ready / Ready**:

| Dimension | Not Ready | Partially Ready | Ready |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Codebase** | Conventions are oral tradition; no architecture docs | Some documentation exists but is incomplete or stale | Architecture, conventions, and patterns are documented and current |
| **Process** | Ad hoc review; manual testing only | Structured review exists; CI covers basics | Automated quality gates, structured review, fast PR cycles |
| **Skill** | Mostly junior team; limited review depth | Mixed seniority; reviewers can catch obvious issues | Strong senior presence; reviewers can evaluate architectural fit |
| **Cultural** | Resistance to tooling change; blame culture | Cautious but open; some voluntary experimentation | Active experimentation; healthy relationship with tooling change |

Assessment questions per dimension:
- **Codebase readiness:** Is there a written architecture document a new hire could use? Are coding standards documented or only enforced in review? Can someone unfamiliar with the project run the build and tests from documentation alone?
- **Process readiness:** Does the team have automated quality gates beyond compilation? Is code review structured or ad hoc? How long does a typical PR take from submission to merge?
- **Skill readiness** (not AI expertise — engineering judgment): What is the ratio of senior to junior developers? Can reviewers articulate *why* code is wrong, not just that it looks wrong? Has the team onboarded a new hire using written documentation in the past year?
- **Cultural readiness:** How did the team respond to the last significant tooling change? Do developers experiment voluntarily, or only when mandated? Is there psychological safety around admitting a tool-assisted approach failed?

The assessment is **not a gate — it is a sequencing tool.** Teams scoring "ready" across all four dimensions are pilot candidates. Teams "partially ready" in one or two dimensions need targeted investment before joining. Teams "not ready" in codebase or skill readiness need the most preparation and should start last.

### Phased Adoption — The Three-Phase Transition Roadmap
The transition from pilot to full adoption follows three phases, each with entry criteria, activities, expected duration, exit signals, and rollback criteria. **Moving to the next phase before exit signals are present is the single most common adoption mistake; ignoring rollback signals is the second.** Durations are ranges, not fixed schedules — organization size and documentation maturity dominate how long each phase actually takes (a 50-person startup with a well-documented codebase moves through Phase 1 in weeks; a 2,000-engineer enterprise with an oral-tradition codebase may need months for the same phase).

**Phase 1: Pilot (1–5 months).** Typical duration: 1–3 months for orgs under 200 engineers; 3–5 months for 200–1,000; 4–6 months for 1,000+. Scale factor is documentation maturity — building even a minimal context layer (project-level instructions, core conventions, architecture boundaries) is, per early-adopter experience, a 4–6 week effort for a team starting from zero documentation, and that work happens *inside* Phase 1, not before it.
- *Objective:* validate that agentic development produces reliable results on your codebase, with your team, under your governance model.
- *Scope:* one or two teams that scored "ready" in the readiness assessment. Limit scope to well-defined work (a new feature, a contained refactor, a test suite expansion) — not a sprawling cross-cutting change. The goal is controlled conditions, not maximum impact.
- *Activities:* establish baseline measurements before the pilot begins (cycle time, review rejection rate, defect rate, developer satisfaction); build the minimum viable context layer (project-level instructions, core coding conventions, architecture boundaries — enough to prevent the most common agent failures, not a comprehensive instrumentation layer); run the pilot with close observation, documenting what agents get right, what they get wrong, and tracking every human intervention point.
- *Exit signals (move to Phase 2 when all hold):* (1) the pilot team has a documented context layer that improves agent output quality; (2) the team has a review workflow for agent-generated code that they trust; (3) baseline and pilot metrics exist for at least four weeks; (4) the pilot team can articulate what worked and what other teams would need.
- *Rollback criteria (pause or restructure after six weeks of active piloting if any hold):* **review rejection rate exceeds 60%** (a calibration starting threshold — your baseline rejection rate should inform the actual trigger; almost always a context quality problem — pause generation work, invest in the context layer, restart the pilot clock); **human intervention rate is not declining week over week** (the first two weeks will be rough; well-scoped pilots see declining intervention by weeks 3–5; a flat or rising line means the team is fighting the tool, not learning from it — diagnose whether the problem is context, skill, or tool fit before continuing); **developer satisfaction drops below pre-pilot baseline** (if people using the tools are less productive or less satisfied than before, the pilot is not validating, it is eroding trust — stop, debrief, determine whether the issue is remediable or fundamental). Rollback process: revert affected teams to their pre-pilot workflow; preserve all context assets and metrics (they are diagnostic, not wasted); conduct a structured retrospective focused on *why* the signals triggered, not *who* is responsible. **Common failure:** declaring the pilot a success based on enthusiasm rather than evidence — "the team loves it" is a data point, not a conclusion; exit signals are structural, not emotional.

**Phase 2: Expand (3–9 months from project start).** Typical duration of Phase 2 itself: 2–4 months for orgs under 200; 3–6 months for 200–1,000; 4–8 months for 1,000+. Scale factor is coaching capacity — the pilot-members-as-coaches model works at this scale but pulls senior engineers off delivery to teach; the coaching model becomes strained beyond a **1:3 ratio** (one coach to three expanding teams) — for organizations expanding beyond five teams, budget for dedicated enablement (a platform engineer, a developer experience lead, or a rotating coaching role).
- *Objective:* extend adoption to additional teams while building the organizational infrastructure (shared context assets, governance processes, skill development) that the pilot didn't require at small scale.
- *Scope:* three to five additional teams, selected based on readiness. Include at least one team that scored "partially ready" in one dimension — this tests whether support infrastructure works for teams that need preparation, not just well-positioned ones.
- *Activities:* pilot team members become internal coaches (the tacit knowledge from Phase 1 is the most valuable asset for Phase 2); build shared context assets (organizational standards, common architectural patterns, cross-project conventions — "the context moat from Chapter 4, the compounding asset that makes every subsequent adoption cheaper"); establish governance processes for agent-generated code at organizational scale (what requires human review, what can be auto-merged with sufficient test coverage, how agent-generated changes are attributed — Chapter 5's framework); begin tracking organizational metrics, not just team metrics.
- *Exit signals (move to Phase 3 when all hold):* (1) expanding teams are productive with agentic tools without daily support from pilot members; (2) shared context assets exist and have a responsible owner; (3) governance processes are documented and followed without enforcement; (4) organizational metrics show a trend you can explain.
- *Rollback criteria (scale back Phase 2 if any hold):* **more than half the expanding teams require daily coach intervention after four weeks** (the support infrastructure is not scaling — pause expansion, reinforce infrastructure, resume with fewer teams); **organizational metrics diverge sharply from pilot metrics** (e.g., the pilot showed a 3:1 generation-to-review ratio and expanding teams are at 1:1 or worse — investigate whether the gap is codebase-specific or structural, i.e. the pilot was a hero team on a friendly codebase); **coach burnout** (if pilot team members are spending roughly a quarter to a third of their time coaching and their own delivery is suffering, it is a capacity problem, not an adoption problem — hire dedicated enablement or slow the expansion rate). **Common failure:** expanding too fast — "the instinct after a successful pilot is to accelerate the rollout," but every team added without readiness or support becomes a negative data point, and negative data points spread faster than positive ones (citing Baumeister et al., "Bad Is Stronger Than Good," *Review of General Psychology*, 2001) — three to five teams in Phase 2 is a **deliberate constraint**.

**Phase 3: Scale (6–24 months from project start).** Typical duration: 3–6 months for orgs under 200; 6–12 months for 200–1,000; 12–24 months for 1,000+. Scale factor is organizational breadth — Phase 3 includes teams that initially scored "not ready" (legacy codebases, thin documentation, mixed enthusiasm), which require more preparation, more coaching, and longer ramp-up; for an organization of 400+ engineers, expect Phase 3 to take at least 12 months, and for 1,000+, plan for 18–24 months — compressing this timeline produces shallow adoption that looks like compliance and functions like resistance.
- *Objective:* make agentic development the default working mode for the engineering organization.
- *Scope:* remaining teams, including those initially scoring "not ready" that have since received preparation.
- *Activities:* onboarding becomes self-service (documentation, shared context assets, and governance processes should be mature enough for a new team to adopt from written resources alone — if not, you are not ready for Phase 3); transition from peer coaching to a dedicated enablement function (a developer experience team, a platform engineering function, or at minimum a named individual responsible for context asset quality, onboarding materials, and tooling support); context engineering becomes a continuous practice, not a one-time setup (stale context is pruned, new patterns are documented as they emerge — Chapter 13 covers this at team scale); metrics mature from "is this working?" to "where do we invest next?" (reducing intervention rates, improving context quality, expanding the range of tasks agents handle reliably); evaluate advanced workflows (multi-agent orchestration, cross-repository operations, CI/CD integration with agent-generated changes) — these require organizational maturity and should not be attempted before Phase 3.
- *Rollback criteria* — explicitly **not all-or-nothing**, but selective: **individual teams showing sustained negative trends** (rising intervention rates, declining satisfaction, increasing rework) after eight weeks of active adoption should pause and receive targeted support — don't force adoption on a team producing worse outcomes with the tools than without; **if organizational-level metrics plateau or decline for a full quarter**, halt further expansion and conduct a systemic review (the most common cause is context asset decay — shared assets that worked for early adopters become stale as more teams rely on them — the fix is investment in maintenance, not more rollout); **kill criteria for the program** — if, after a full Phase 1 and Phase 2 cycle, fewer than **40%** of participating teams show measurable improvement on quality or efficiency metrics (a suggested threshold, adjustable to your organization's risk tolerance), the transition is not producing organizational value. This does not mean the tools are useless — it may mean the codebase, team composition, or domain is not yet a good fit. Document what you learned, preserve what worked at the team level, and revisit when conditions change. **"A transition plan without a kill switch is an escalation of commitment."**
- *Exit signal:* Phase 3 does not have an endpoint — it is the steady state. The signal is not that everyone is using the tools; it is that the tools produce measurable value, the organization can sustain and improve that value, and the transition is no longer a "project" but an ongoing capability.

### Metrics That Matter
"Measure what predicts long-term value, not what flatters short-term adoption." A consolidated metrics table across four categories plus DORA:

| Category | Metric | What It Tells You | What It Doesn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| Quality | Review rejection rate for agent-generated PRs | Whether agents are producing code your team trusts | Whether the accepted code has latent defects |
| Quality | Human intervention rate per task | How often agents need correction mid-task | Why they need correction (context gap? tool limitation?) |
| Efficiency | Time-to-confident-merge | End-to-end time from task start to merged, reviewed code | Whether faster merges translate to faster feature delivery |
| Efficiency | Rework rate on agent-generated code (30-day) | Whether agent output survives contact with production | What the rework costs (trivial fixes vs. architectural changes) |
| Adoption | Context asset coverage | What percentage of your codebase has structured context | Whether that context is good (coverage without quality is noise) |
| Adoption | Active usage rate vs. license count | Whether people who have the tools are using them | Whether they're using them well |
| DORA | Deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR | Baseline delivery performance and trends | Causation — many factors affect DORA metrics simultaneously |

Start with **DORA metrics** as the shared language — well-understood, widely adopted, and they provide a baseline that predates agentic adoption. Then add the agent-specific metrics (intervention rate, rejection rate, rework rate) as leading indicators of whether the tools are producing genuine value or shifting effort between phases.

**The single most important metric most organizations do not track: the generation-to-review ratio** — the ratio of time spent *generating* code with agents to time spent *reviewing and correcting* agent-generated code. If that ratio is improving (less time reviewing per unit of generation), the tools are working. If it is flat or worsening, there is a context quality problem, a skill problem, or both. Three levels of instrumenting it, from least to most investment:
- **Level 1: PR metadata (low effort, moderate accuracy).** Tag agent-generated PRs with a label (`agent-generated`, `ai-assisted`); measure creation time (proxy for generation end) and final approval time (proxy for review end); ratio = aggregate review time ÷ aggregate generation time across tagged PRs. Noisy (PRs vary in size, review includes non-agent concerns) but produces a directional trend. Tools: GitHub labels + a weekly query against PR analytics.
- **Level 2: Annotation tags in workflow (medium effort, good accuracy).** Developers annotate task tracking with structured tags (`[gen-start]`, `[gen-end]`, `[review-start]`, `[review-end]`) — as lightweight as a comment in the task tracker or a structured field in project management tooling. Overhead is roughly 30 seconds per task; accuracy improves because you measure developer time, not PR lifecycle time.
- **Level 3: Git hooks and editor telemetry (higher effort, high accuracy).** A pre-commit hook detects agent-generated code (via agent metadata, co-author trailers, or configurable markers) and logs generation events; editor extensions track time in "generation mode" vs. "review mode" based on active tool state, feeding a dashboard that computes the ratio automatically. This is **Phase 3 investment — do not attempt it in the pilot.**

**What healthy looks like** (starting benchmarks based on author observation of early adopter teams, not industry-validated thresholds — calibrate against your own baseline): early in adoption, expect a generation-to-review ratio around **1:1** (an hour of review and correction per hour of agent-assisted generation); as context quality improves and teams calibrate delegation patterns, healthy teams reach **3:1 or better**. **Below 1.5:1 after four weeks of active use** indicates a context quality problem — agents are producing output that costs almost as much to verify as it saves in creation. **Above 5:1** warrants scrutiny in the other direction — verify review rigor hasn't declined along with review time.

### Common Transition Pitfalls
Six predictable and preventable patterns that derail transitions:
1. **The premature rollout.** Scaling before the pilot has produced foundational lessons (a working context layer, a validated review process, metrics that show a trend). Premature acceleration typically costs multiples of the time saved, in later remediation, when unprepared teams have bad experiences.
2. **The mandate without infrastructure.** Leadership announces all teams will use agentic tools by Q3 — no investment in context engineering, no training, no governance updates. Developers receive a tool and a deadline. Adoption is shallow and resentful.
3. **The wrong metric.** Measuring lines of code, PR volume, or tool usage frequency instead of quality and effectiveness metrics. Teams optimize for whatever is measured, and optimizing for volume with generative AI tools produces more code, not better software.
4. **The hero pilot.** The pilot team includes your three best developers and a greenfield project. The pilot succeeds brilliantly. Nothing learned transfers to a team of mixed seniority working on a legacy codebase. Select pilot teams that are representative, not exceptional.
5. **The missing middle.** Investing in executive strategy and practitioner tools but not in organizational connective tissue: shared context assets, coaching capacity, governance processes. The gap between "leadership approves" and "developers succeed" is filled by middle management, team leads, and staff engineers — if they are not equipped, the transition stalls.
6. **The permanence assumption.** Treating agentic development as a one-time transformation rather than an ongoing practice. Context goes stale. Tools evolve. Team composition changes. The transition is not a project with a completion date — it is the beginning of a continuous capability that requires continuous investment.

## Important

### Skill Development Paths
Different roles need different skills — "a one-size-fits-all training program wastes time for everyone":
- **Senior engineers and tech leads** need context engineering skills: how to build and design instruction hierarchies, how to evaluate agent output against architectural requirements, and how to recognize the failure modes from Chapter 14 — not because they'll hit every one, but because recognizing a failure mode early prevents hours of debugging. This is the **highest-priority training investment**.
- **Mid-level developers** need effective delegation skills: how to scope tasks for agents, provide sufficient context for a specific interaction, and iterate when agent output is wrong rather than starting over. They also need calibrated trust — understanding when agent output is likely reliable versus when it requires careful verification. The PROSE framework (Chapter 10) provides the methodology.
- **Junior developers** need review skills first, generation skills second. The most dangerous scenario is a junior developer who accepts agent output they cannot evaluate. Before juniors use agentic tools for generation, they should be able to review agent-generated code at the same standard they review human code. Pairing juniors with seniors during initial agent-assisted work is not optional — "it is a safety measure."
- **Engineering managers** need measurement and coaching skills: how to evaluate whether a team's adoption is productive, how to identify when developers are struggling, and how to create an environment where admitting "the agent's approach didn't work" is acceptable. The cultural readiness dimension is primarily their responsibility.
- **Architects and staff engineers** need to understand how agentic development changes system design. When agents participate in implementation, the value of explicit interfaces, clear module boundaries, and documented architectural decisions increases — implicit architecture (the kind that lives in the heads of the people who built it) becomes a liability. Their path focuses on making architecture **agent-legible**, which also makes it more maintainable by humans.

### Transition Planning Template
A checklist-format template spanning Pre-Transition through Ongoing, explicitly "a starting point, not a specification":

**Pre-Transition (Weeks 1–4):** conduct readiness assessments for all candidate teams; establish baseline metrics (DORA + quality) for pilot candidates; identify pilot team(s) — one to two teams scoring "ready" across all dimensions; define pilot scope with specific workstreams and clear boundaries; assign an executive sponsor and transition lead; determine tool selection based on the evaluation framework (Chapter 2); review governance requirements (Chapter 5) and identify minimum viable policies.

**Phase 1 — Pilot (1–5 months, depending on org size and documentation maturity):** build minimum viable context layer for the pilot team's codebase (Chapters 8–9); train the pilot team on context engineering basics and agent interaction patterns; begin the pilot with structured observation, logging intervention points, failure modes, and successes; conduct weekly retrospectives focused on what agents get right and wrong; collect pilot metrics for at least four consecutive weeks; monitor rollback signals at week six (rejection rate, intervention trend, developer satisfaction); document lessons learned — what other teams need to know before starting; evaluate Phase 1 exit signals before proceeding.

**Phase 2 — Expand (3–9 months from start, depending on org size):** select three to five expansion teams based on readiness; assign pilot team members as coaches (budget for dedicated enablement if expanding beyond five teams); build shared context assets — organizational standards, architectural patterns, cross-project conventions; establish governance processes for agent-generated code; begin role-specific skill development; track organizational metrics; monitor rollback signals — coach dependency, metric divergence, capacity strain; conduct monthly cross-team retrospectives; evaluate Phase 2 exit signals before proceeding.

**Phase 3 — Scale (6–24 months from start, depending on org size):** extend to remaining teams with self-service onboarding; transition from peer coaching to a dedicated enablement function; establish continuous context maintenance (ownership, review, pruning); mature metrics from adoption tracking to effectiveness optimization; evaluate advanced workflows — multi-agent orchestration, CI/CD integration; assign permanent ownership of context assets and governance; monitor per-team rollback signals, pausing individual teams showing sustained negative trends; report organizational impact using the metrics framework.

**Ongoing:** review context asset quality quarterly; update skill development as tools and practices evolve; reassess governance policies as agent capabilities expand; track the generation-to-review time ratio as the primary health indicator.

## Key Terms

- **Productivity Paradox** — the Chapter 3 concept that traditional velocity/LOC metrics mislead during AI-assisted transition because they measure output volume, not outcome quality; the reason this chapter plans around readiness level instead.
- **Generation-to-review ratio** — time spent generating code with agents divided by time spent reviewing/correcting it; the single most important untracked metric, with healthy mature teams reaching 3:1 or better and below 1.5:1 signaling a context quality problem.
- **Readiness assessment** — a four-dimension (codebase, process, skill, cultural) sequencing tool, not a gate, used to decide which teams pilot first and which need preparation.
- **Rollback criteria** — explicit, pre-defined thresholds (e.g., review rejection rate >60%, flat/rising intervention rate, satisfaction below pre-pilot baseline) that trigger pausing or reverting a phase, distinct from "exit signals" that permit advancing.
- **Kill criteria** — the threshold (suggested: fewer than 40% of participating teams showing measurable improvement after a full Phase 1+2 cycle) at which the transition itself should be reconsidered, not just individual teams.
- **Hero pilot** — the common pitfall of staffing a pilot with the best developers on a greenfield project, producing results that don't transfer to representative teams on real codebases.
- **DORA metrics** — deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR; recommended as the shared baseline language before layering on agent-specific metrics.
